Criterion A – Investigating

Themes, Symbolism & Visual Culture

Statement of Investigation

Happiness can be derived from and shared through communal visuals, especially in visual culture where symbols, colours, and design choices communicate emotional meaning and identity.

Unit Purpose & Key Concepts

Key Concept: Communication

Related Concepts: Visual culture, Interpretation

Global Context: Identities and Relationships

Factual Question 1 — What symbolic meaning might be read into happiness?

Happiness can be symbolized in many ways through colours, shapes, textures, and cultural motifs. A sunrise may represent new beginnings, while fireworks show shared joy. Colours like yellow signal warmth and cheerfulness, while green often symbolizes growth and peace. Shapes such as hearts, circles, and butterflies communicate joy, love, and transformation. In fashion and visual culture, happiness is expressed through design choices like crystals, flowing fabrics, and bright palettes...

Factual Question 2 — Where in our lives can we use non-verbal communication of emotions?

Non-verbal communication appears everywhere—from facial expressions to body posture, rituals, fashion, and digital emojis. A dancer’s movement, a painting’s brushstroke, or a glowing gown can express emotions without words. Designers like Phạm Đăng Anh Thư communicate identity and confidence through texture, light, and silhouette...

Conceptual Question 1 — What is happiness and how do we define it?

Happiness can mean well-being, balance, success, belonging, or creativity. It shifts across people and societies. Fashion often expresses happiness as empowerment and confidence, like when someone wears a crystal-covered gown...

Conceptual Question 2 — How does society respond to emotional well-being?

Some cultures support emotional well-being openly, while others stigmatize it. Visual culture helps bridge gaps by allowing emotions to be expressed symbolically through clothing, art, or colour. People act on emotional well-being by fostering empathy, celebrating creativity, and supporting mental health spaces...

Conceptual Question 3 — How does visual culture communicate values?

On social media, emojis, digital art, and photography spread values quickly. In real life, fashion shows, murals, textiles, and rituals communicate identity and cultural meaning. A Swarovski-embroidered dress or a public mural might symbolize unity, joy, or hope...

Debatable Question — How do cultures define happiness differently?

Happiness varies across cultures. Collectivist cultures tie happiness to family, unity, and tradition, while individualist cultures tie it to independence and self-expression. In Asia, phoenix symbolism represents rebirth, while in Western couture, bold jeweled corsets may symbolize power or confidence...

Art Movement — Contemporary Couture

Time Period: Early 2000s

Origin: Paris

Influences: Chanel, Dior, McQueen, Vietnamese symbolism, Finnish optimism

Major Artists: Phạm Đăng Anh Thư, Alexander McQueen

Characteristics: flowing fabrics, sculptural silhouettes, heavy embellishment, symbolic meaning

Case Study — Phạm Đăng Anh Thư

Anh Thư blends Vietnamese cultural symbolism with haute couture craftsmanship. Her Ice Phoenix gown combines satin, organza layers, and Swarovski crystals to represent rebirth, empowerment, and emotional transformation. The gradient colours and phoenix symbolism strongly influence this project’s visual language...

Case Study — Marimekko

Marimekko’s bold patterns, especially the Unikko poppy print, express collective happiness and Scandinavian optimism. Their focus on everyday joy and accessible art contrasts with couture but helped shape the project’s approach to colour, repetition, and visual communication...

Comparative Analysis

Anh Thư: symbolic motifs, gradients, sculptural forms

Marimekko: repetition, colour harmony, bold patterning

Together, they show how personal transformation and collective joy can both be expressed visually.

Conclusion

Contemporary couture and textile design demonstrate how visual culture communicates happiness across identities and cultures. Through symbolism, colour psychology, and storytelling, art becomes a universal language connecting people.
